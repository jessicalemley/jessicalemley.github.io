My challenge was to give members of a cryptocurrency fund a simple way to monitor the performance of an automated trading bot and the growth of their portfolios.

Challenge

My client was an android software developer and cryptocurrency trader who was building an automated trading bot that would manage an investment fund for himself and a small group of users. The goal was to find a way for fund members keep an eye on the bot's performance without revealing the formula it used to make decisions.

Discover

We started by documenting his intended users (friends, family, and coworkers), challenges with existing solutions, and his goals for the bot and dashboard.

Given that the solution was intended to be a privately used application, we had a relatively small pool of user types to consider. Still, we put together some interview questions and discussed the idea of the bot with his intended customers to gauge interest, gather feedback, and test our initial hypotheses around the value prop.

We also looked at competitors such as Tradeview, Coinbase, and traditional investment dashboards to learn how people used these tools and what their pain points were. Then we created an affinity diagram to discover common themes and patterns.

Common Insights

Avid crypto users :

  • Anxiety over market volatility and its impact on personal wealth.
  • Defiant optimism about the future of Bitcoin as a valuable commodity and tansactional medium.
  • Fear of "missing out" on positive price swings.
  • With a few notable exceptions, users had between $10,000 and $50,000 invested in cryptocurrency.
  • Obsessively monitored exchange accounts and market data.
  • Irritated with low, slow returns and high fees with existing fund managers.
  • Don't have time or interest to learn all these trading strategies.
  • Common buzzwords/phrases: "FUD" "HODL" "To the moon!" "Now's the time to buy..."

Non-crypto users:

  • General dismissive or distrustful feelings toward cryptocurrency (some eye rolls were recorded).
  • Readily admitted they were not sure what it is or how it works.
  • Concerns that it is a scam or a fad.
  • Uncertain how secure it is.
  • Turned off by complicated purchasing, trading, and analytics.
  • Afraid of getting "hacked."

When it came to investments, both users prioritized the same primary concerns:

  • Am I making money?
  • Is the return worth the cost?
  • Does the return outweigh the risk?

If the answer to all of these was "yes," they all felt their fears and anxieties would be alleviated.

Define

Personas

From there we were able to come up with two personas: (Primary) an avid crypto user who regularly purchases Bitcoin and obsessively checks the market price throughout the day, and (Secondary) a non-crypto user who is looking for a "set-it-and-forget-it" way to grow their wealth without a lot of work and worry.

Defining the problem

Our conversations with both types of users underscored the fact that they wanted to keep a close and constant eye on what their money was doing. Our interviews also confirmed that they wanted to grow their accounts quickly and be able to take advantage of sporadic market upticks.

Based on the information we gathered, we decided that for our MVP we would focus on the primary persona and design a tool that would allow them to watch the bot's activities in real-time while also tracking their portfolio's performance metrics.

Ideate

Now it was time to come up with a way to solve the problem. One UX challenge we had was that the bot was going to be hardware based so that use of the algorithm would limited only to those who were given a bot box and access credentials.This pretty much eliminated the options of a mobile or web application. Fortunately, we learned during the Discovery phase that most of our primary crypto users monitored the markets throughout the day by keeping an exchange tab open on a computer or TV monitor, so we decided to design an interface that was optimized this type of viewing.

We started by sketching a number of different ideas, some of which can be seen below, and iterated on the designs until we had a refined concept that we felt best accomplished our users' goals.

Super Simple Dashboards

The idea we were most happy with was a two-page TV dashboard that gave users a real-time overview of their bot's activity and also allowed them to monitor and compare their portfolio's performance over time. We wanted to give our user's the ability to see how their funds are doing at-a-glance, so we stuck to high-level KPI's and removed the clutter of typical exchanges and wallet dashboards.

I built on this idea and created more detailed wireframes of the interface to validate the usability of the layout, then used those structures to create high-fidelity mock ups.

UX Challenges

This project represented some unique challenges in terms of design and experience.

  1. Navigational Limitations

    TV UIs are controlled by a D-pad and used at a distance from the screen. The D-pad limits navigation to four direction: up, down, left and right. Unlike web-based dashboards, we could not use nested interactive content.

  2. Legibility

    TV screens are viewed from a greater distance than mobile and desktop screens. Legibility becomes an important feature which means that the size of text and other elements must be adjusted accordingly. We had to figure out how to communicate a lot of information in while keeping the data legible.

  3. Color

    TV screens treat color differently. "Hot" colors like saturated yellows and reds tend to bleed and bright whites create halos and flickering. Big white backgrounds feel blinding to TV users and even hurt your eyes in low light. So we needed to make our visual indicators bright enough to stand out without causing discomfort or distraction.

  4. Accessibility

    Speaking of colors, the most commonly used colors in visual indicators on crypto charts and dashboards are red and green. Of course, this is the absolutely worst color combination for those on the colorblindness spectrum. We had to find colors that communicated positive and negative values effectively but also allowed provided a great experience for colorblind users.

Prototype

Splash Screen

Real-Time Dashboard

When the app loads, you are taken to the Real-Time dashboard where you can view your bot's activity overlayed on a market chart. Color-blind-friendly chart indicators show when the bot buys, sells, and holds Bitcoin. Bear and Bull icons on the chart represent whether the market is on an upward or downward trend. You can also quickly see their portfolio's collective value and growth in USD, view their bot's performance over a one week rolling period. Finally, you can watch the bot's real-time holdings and active orders complete with profit/loss calculations.

Portfolio Dashboard

"Am I Making Money?"

In discovery, we learned that typical market analysis platforms display performance metrics in a strategy performance report. To help our users better evaluate the bot's performance, we included five key performance metrics on the dashboard:

  1. Total Net Profit
  2. Profit Factor
  3. Percent Profitable
  4. Average Trade Net Profit
  5. Maximum Drawdown

Additionally, many of our users cashed out their Bitcoin to pay for living expenses or large purchases. Since this impacts their overall portfolio value and illustrates how the bot is supplementing income, we included a payout tracker.

"Is the Return Worth the Cost?"

Operating the bot will require the fund manager to pay subscription costs to access data feeds. There are also Miner fees that are assessed with every trade. The Fees tile allows you to see how these costs impact your total portfolio value.

"Is the Return Worth the Risk?"

Finally, the Portfolio dashboard charts your bot's performance against the what you would have made using the "HODL" (buy and hold) strategy. You can also see periods of drawdown; times when the bot traded for a loss.

Test

The next step will be to bring the screens back to our group of our primary users and conduct a test scenario in which we ask them to tell us how they would answer their three main questions using the dashboard:

  • Am I making money?
  • Is the return worth the cost?
  • Does the return outweigh the risk?

We'll collect the results of these challenges as well as any other feedback and observations and use those findings to further refine the design and inform future feature decisions.
